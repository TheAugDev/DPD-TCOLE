Multiculturalism & Human Relations

Principles for Fair and Effective Policing

I. Understanding Prejudice and Bias

The Nature of Prejudice

Prejudice is a negative opinion formed without knowledge or facts, based instead on assumptions. While most people try to avoid it, everyone holds some form of prejudice. It is better to acknowledge and address these biases than to pretend they don't exist.

Roots of Prejudice:

  • Ethnocentrism: The belief that one's own group or culture is superior to others.
  • Stereotyping: Generalizing about a group of people without regard for individual differences, often out of laziness to learn the truth.
  • Scapegoating: Blaming other people or groups for one's own problems or failings.

The Mandate for Impartiality

Because peace officers often work alone and have significant discretion, avoiding even the appearance of bias is critical. Officers must commit to the highest ethical standards.

  • No Favoritism: An officer must never favor one person over another in the same situation.
  • Equal Treatment: Officers must indicate through both words and actions that no one will receive preferential treatment.
  • Resist Temptation: Officers must remain impartial even when acting with bias might seem to produce a "better" or easier outcome, or when they believe biased behavior would go unpunished.

II. Practicing Sensitivity and Cross-Cultural Communication

The Power of Sensitivity

Policing is more effective when officers are sensitive to the emotions of the citizens they serve. This builds trust and cooperation.

  • Be Nonjudgmental: See yourself as a detached enforcer of the law.
  • Be Empathetic: Strive to understand and honor the feelings of others.
  • Be Friendly: Warmth and compassion reap rewards in cooperation and assistance from the community, while a lack of sensitivity can breed ill will and violence.

Cross-Cultural Conflict Resolution

Officers must be adept at communicating across diverse cultures, as communication styles can vary significantly. Police behavior that is normal in one culture may be offensive in another.

Example of Cultural Difference:

In many Middle-Eastern cultures, hospitality is very important. An officer should avoid entering a home uninvited and, if possible, remove their shoes. This formality would not necessarily be expected when entering a Native-American home, demonstrating the need for specific cultural knowledge.
