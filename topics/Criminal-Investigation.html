Criminal Investigation

From Crime Scene to Courtroom

I. Foundations of Investigation

The Three Pillars of Investigation

A successful criminal investigation stands on three core pillars:

  • Information Gathering: The most critical element. This involves collecting details from victims, witnesses, suspects, and official records.
  • Forensic Science: The application of scientific methods like DNA analysis, fingerprinting, ballistics, and serology to analyze evidence.
  • Laws of Arrest, Search, and Seizure: The legal framework (Fourth Amendment, CCP) that governs all investigative protocols to ensure evidence is admissible in court.

Criminal Typologies

Professional Criminals

  • Thief: Steals for money, usually with a plan but less stealthy than a burglar.
  • Burglar: Breaks into buildings with stealth and skill, often works with a "fence" to sell stolen goods.
  • Robber: Uses violence or the threat of violence to take property directly from a person.

Other Offender Types

  • Con Artist: Uses deception and false promises to trick people out of money.
  • Semi-Professional Thief: Steals sporadically out of need, with less planning and a higher likelihood of resorting to violence.
  • White-Collar Criminal: Commits nonviolent financial offenses like fraud or embezzlement.

II. Investigating Specific Crimes

Homicide Investigation

The initial response is critical: aid the injured, secure the scene, and identify witnesses. Investigators must quickly determine the time, place, cause, and means of death.

Common Homicide Motives:

  • Felony Murder: Occurs during the commission of another serious crime (e.g., robbery).
  • Anger Killing: Not premeditated, often resembling a highly violent assault.
  • Triangle Killing: Involves a cheating spouse and their partner.
  • Sadistic Murder: Involves torture or extreme cruelty, often sexual in nature.

Sexual Assault Investigation

These cases are extremely delicate. The officer's first priority is to care for and comfort the victim. Evidence collection and interviews must be done with sensitivity. The general intention of sexual assault is a demonstration of power.

Typology of Rapists:

  • Sadistic Rapist: Finds violence sexually arousing; crimes are often prolonged and involve degradation.
  • Anger Rapist: Uses rape to punish and express rage; the act is often brief and violent.
  • Power Rapist: Seeks to create a feeling of personal agency and control; may even apologize afterward.
  • Opportunistic Rapist: Does not plan the crime; takes advantage of an incapacitated victim.

Crimes Against Children & the Elderly

These investigations require special care for vulnerable victims.

  • Child Abuse: Abusive parents often have a history of being abused, may have substance abuse issues, and may perceive their child as "evil." Children react to violence differently based on age, from regression and bed-wetting to aggression.
  • Elder Abuse: Often takes the form of neglect, financial exploitation, or physical/emotional abuse. These cases are difficult because victims may be reluctant or unable to provide information.

Auto Theft Investigation

Auto theft can range from joyriding to sophisticated professional operations.

In The Field: Identifying Stolen Cars

  • Broken window or damaged steering column.
  • License plate doesn't match VIN, is dirty, or held on by magnets/string.
  • Driver is nervous, has no ID, or refuses to turn off the car.

III. Scene Processing & Evidence

Crime Scene Search Patterns

Strip Search: Searching in parallel lines, best for large outdoor areas.
Circular Search: Searching in concentric circles from a central point, good for finding a specific missing object.
Quadrant Search: Dividing the scene into sections, useful for areas with natural landmarks.
Point-to-Point: Moving from one piece of obvious evidence to the next. Good starting point but lacks organization.

Documenting the Scene

Proper documentation is vital for court. This includes:

  • Photography: Should move from exterior to interior, and from long-range to close-up shots of evidence. A case information card should be the first photo.
  • Sketches: Provide a map of the scene and show relationships between objects. Can be perspective, projection (3D), or schematic (showing a path of events).
  • Measurements: Use triangulation or rectangulation to fix the position of evidence.

Evidence Handling & Chain of Custody

The Chain of Custody is a detailed log of everyone who has handled a piece of evidence. It is essential to prove the evidence has not been tampered with. Evidence must be carefully packed in separate containers, and wet items like bloodstains must be air-dried before packaging to prevent degradation.

Key Term Flashcards

Corpus Delicti

(Click to Flip)

Latin for "body of the crime." The concrete evidence of a crime, such as a dead body or damaged property.

Modus Operandi (M.O.)

(Click to Flip)

A criminal's characteristic method of operation or "style" of committing a crime.

Circumstantial Evidence

(Click to Flip)

Evidence that suggests a fact by implication or inference, rather than directly proving it (e.g., a suspect's lack of an alibi).

Reasonable Doubt

(Click to Flip)

The standard of proof required for a jury to acquit a defendant in a criminal trial. It's a doubt based on reason and common sense.

Rigor Mortis

(Click to Flip)

The stiffening of body tissues after death, which can help in estimating the time of death.

Luminol

(Click to Flip)

A chemical used at crime scenes to detect trace amounts of blood, even if it has been cleaned or is invisible to the naked eye.

Chain of Custody

(Click to Flip)

A chronological paper trail showing the seizure, custody, control, transfer, analysis, and disposition of evidence.

Complainant

(Click to Flip)

The person who reports a crime or demands that a criminal investigation be initiated.
