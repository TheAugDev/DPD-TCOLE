Strategies of Defense: Mechanics of Arrest

Tactics for Control, Safety, and Legal Justification

I. Use of Force & Weapon Systems

Weaponless Strategies

These strategies require excellent body control, coordination, and situational awareness. Key elements include:

  • Situational Awareness: Being aware of the suspect's body, potential weapons, accomplices, and escape routes.
  • Verbal Communication: Constant communication with the suspect and fellow officers is essential.
  • Safe Distance: Maintain enough distance to observe furtive movements and react to threats.
  • Common Methods: Joint-locking, pressure points, hand/foot strikes, and blocks with hands, arms, or legs.

Impact Weapons (Batons)

Impact weapons like straight, expandable, or side-handle batons are appropriate when force is necessary but deadly force is not justified.

When to Use:

  • A suspect fails to comply with verbal commands.
  • An officer is assaulted by an unarmed suspect.
  • An officer is assaulted by a suspect with a weapon other than a firearm.
  • During crowd control as part of a tactical squad.

Targeting and Prohibitions:

  • Target Areas: Meaty areas of the arms, legs, or the abdomen.
  • Prohibited Areas: Never intentionally strike the head, throat, chest, groin, or center of the back.
  • Stance: Maintain a defensive posture, holding the baton between the officer and the suspect.

Deadly Force

Deadly force should only be used as a last resort when an officer's life or the life of another person is under direct and imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury. Deadly force can be applied with firearms, control weapons, vehicles, or even empty hands.

II. The Arrest Process: From Approach to Transport

The Decision to Arrest

An officer uses judgment and discretion to decide on the necessity of an arrest, considering factors like:

  • Suspect's Behavior: Does the suspect have a behavioral disorder? Are they intoxicated?
  • Time and Location: Suspects are more likely to flee or conceal weapons at night.
  • Presence of Others: The presence of spectators or associates may require a quick arrest to de-escalate the situation.

Suspect Approach Tactics

How an officer approaches a suspect can determine the outcome of the encounter.

  • Direct Approach: Allows full observation of the suspect's movements but gives them time to prepare an attack or escape.
  • Rear Approach: Allows for surprise but can cause a violent or unpredictable reaction.
  • Side Approach: A compromise, but limits the officer's ability to see all of the suspect's actions (e.g., their other hand).
  • Two-Officer Approach: One officer should take the lead (contact officer) while the second observes (cover officer). They should not approach from the same side.

Handcuffing Techniques

Proper handcuffing is a critical skill for officer and suspect safety.

  • Standard Application: Bring hands behind the back, palms facing out, keyholes up.
  • Double-Lock: Handcuffs MUST be double-locked to prevent them from tightening and to make them more difficult to pick.
  • Two Suspects, One Cuff: Cuff right hand to right hand OR left hand to left hand to impede their ability to run.
  • Never Too Tight: Cuffs applied too tightly can cause injury and lead to charges of excessive force.

Searching & Transporting Suspects

Physical Search Positions: A suspect can be searched while standing, kneeling (legs crossed, fingers interlaced behind head), or prone (lying down with arms and legs spread). Always control the suspect's hands.

Transporting (No Cage):

  • One Officer, One Suspect: Suspect in right front seat, seatbelted, doors locked. Officer's weapon is on their left side, away from the suspect.
  • Two Officers, One Suspect: Suspect in right rear seat. One officer drives, the other sits in the left rear seat behind the driver.
