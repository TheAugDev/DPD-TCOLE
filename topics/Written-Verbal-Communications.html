Written and Verbal Communications

The Foundation of Effective Policing and Reporting

I. Fundamentals of Effective Writing

The Priority of Clarity

In police work, written communication is not about style or ornamentation; the absolute priority is clarity. Reports and notes must be easily understood by others, as the author may not be available to explain them. To achieve clarity, officers must:

  • Write in Complete Sentences: Every sentence must have a subject and a verb and express a complete thought.
  • Avoid Sentence Fragments: Incomplete sentences that lack a subject or verb.
  • Avoid Run-on Sentences: Long, confusing sentences that should be broken up.
  • Use the Active Voice: The active voice (e.g., "The officer arrested the suspect.") is more direct and clearer than the passive voice (e.g., "The suspect was arrested by the officer.").
  • Avoid Slang and Jargon: Use plain, professional language that anyone can understand.
  • Be Precise and Concise: Convey all relevant information without unnecessary words or repetition.

The Major Parts of Speech

A basic understanding of grammar is essential for clear report writing.

  • Noun: Names a person, place, or thing (e.g., officer, street, handcuff).
  • Pronoun: Stands in for a noun (e.g., he, she, it, they).
  • Verb: Describes an action or state of being (e.g., ran, stopped, was).
  • Adjective: Modifies (describes) a noun (e.g., a *red* car, a *tall* suspect).
  • Adverb: Modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb (e.g., he ran *quickly*).
  • Preposition: Links a noun to another word (e.g., the suspect was *in* the car).

II. Field Notes and Official Reports

The Importance of Field Notes

Field notes are brief records of an officer's observations taken at the scene. They are invaluable for several reasons:

  • Foundation of Reports: They provide the raw data for creating a detailed and accurate official report.
  • Memory Aid: They capture small details that might otherwise be forgotten, especially in fast-moving or traumatic situations.
  • Reduces Follow-up: Effective notes reduce the need to re-contact witnesses or victims for information.
  • Accuracy: They provide greater accuracy regarding the time, sequence, and character of important events.

Information to Record: From Field Notes to Report

An officer must gather specific, detailed information for a report to be complete and useful for an investigation.

Person Descriptions

  • Full Name & Aliases
  • Race & Gender
  • Height & Weight
  • Hair & Eye Color
  • Complexion, Scars, Tattoos
  • Clothing
  • Contact Information (Address, Phone)

Vehicle & Property

  • CYMBAL: Color, Year, Make, Body Style, License Plate Number
  • Vehicle Identification Number (VIN)
  • Property: Manufacturer, model, serial number, value, color, etc.

Essential Report Narrative Information

Beyond descriptions, a complete report must include: the date, time, and location of the offense; the nature of the crime; possible motives; how the offense occurred; and how the officer became involved.

Key Term Flashcards

Brief, contemporaneous records of an officer's observations and actions at a scene, used as the foundation for official reports.

Active Voice

(Click to Flip)

A sentence structure where the subject performs the action (e.g., "The officer interviewed the witness."). It is preferred for clarity in police reports.

Passive Voice

(Click to Flip)

A sentence structure where the subject receives the action (e.g., "The witness was interviewed by the officer."). It should be avoided as it is less direct.

CYMBAL

(Click to Flip)

The standard acronym for gathering and broadcasting vehicle descriptions: Color, Year, Make/Model, Body style, And License plate number.
