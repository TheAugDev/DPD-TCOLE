Arrest, Search, and Seizure

Mastering the Fourth Amendment Standards

Core Concepts & Scenarios

The Spectrum of Proof

Understanding the different levels of proof is critical, as each level justifies a different degree of police intrusion on a citizen's liberty.

Mere Suspicion / Hunch

A gut feeling with no supporting facts. This does not allow for any detention. It only justifies continued observation and voluntary conversation, during which the citizen is always free to leave.

Reasonable Suspicion (Terry v. Ohio)

A standard based on specific, articulable facts that suggest criminal activity is occurring. It allows for a temporary, investigative detention (Terry Stop). During this stop, a person is not required to identify themselves or answer questions. A frisk is only permissible if there is also reasonable suspicion the person is armed.

Probable Cause

The highest standard of proof for an officer in the field. It's a conclusion based on a totality of circumstances that would lead a reasonable person to believe a crime has been committed. It is required to make an arrest or obtain a search warrant.

Building and Applying Probable Cause

Probable cause is not built from a single factor, but from the totality of the circumstances. An officer can use their training and experience to interpret these factors.

Factors Contributing to PC:

  • Furtive Acts: Secretive or evasive behavior.
  • Officer's Knowledge: Prior history of the suspect.
  • Informant's Tip: Information from a credible source.
  • Location: High-crime area.
  • Time of Day: Unusual time for the observed activity.
  • Possession: Proximity to contraband or tools of a crime.

Frisks vs. Searches

While both are intrusions under the Fourth Amendment, their legal justification, purpose, and scope are distinctly different.

Frisk (Pat-Down)

  • Purpose: Officer safety (to find weapons).
  • Justification: Reasonable suspicion that the subject is armed and dangerous.
  • Scope: Limited to the outer clothing; not a search for evidence.

Search

  • Purpose: To find evidence of a crime, contraband, fruits of a crime, or tools of a crime.
  • Justification: Probable cause.
  • Scope: Can be extensive, defined by a warrant or by exceptions like search incident to arrest.

Relevant Legal Codes

CCP Art. 18.16 - Preventing Consequences of Theft

Grants citizens the right to seize stolen property and the person who stole it, provided they act immediately and take both before a magistrate or peace officer. This is a rare instance of a "citizen's arrest" being codified for a specific purpose.

CCP Art. 51.13 - Uniform Criminal Extradition Act

Allows a peace officer or private citizen to perform a warrantless arrest if they have reasonable information that the accused has committed a felony in another state (punishable by at least one year in prison). The accused must be taken before a magistrate as soon as possible.

CCP Art. 15.04 & 15.05 - Complaint

A complaint is a sworn affidavit charging an offense. It must contain the accused's name/description, the offense details (time/place), and the affiant's signature.
