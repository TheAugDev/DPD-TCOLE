Emergency Communications

Protocols for Clarity and Precision

I. Principles of Effective Communication

The Importance of Precision

In an emergency, every word matters. Communications must be clear, concise, and accurate to ensure a proper response and maintain officer safety.

  • Audience: Broadcasts should be made over channels that reach the largest and most appropriate audience.
  • Urgency: Emergency channels should be reserved for the most extreme circumstances to prevent the public from becoming desensitized ("tuning out").
  • Clarity: Messages must clearly state they are for an emergency to command attention immediately.
  • Timing: Broadcasts should be delivered at times that maximize the potential audience and impact.

Standardized Broadcast Information

Following a standard order for descriptions ensures that information is transmitted efficiently and can be easily understood by all listening parties.

Person Description

  1. Name
  2. Gender
  3. Race
  4. Date of Birth
  5. Height / Weight
  6. Hair / Eye Color
  7. Complexion, Markings, Clothing (as additional details)

Vehicle Description (CYMBAL)

  1. Color
  2. Year
  3. Make / Model
  4. Body Style
  5. And
  6. License Plate Number

II. Radio Discipline and Technique

Effective Radio Broadcasting

How a message is delivered is as important as what it says. An effective broadcaster demonstrates professionalism and ensures the message is understood.

  • Voice Control: Project your voice clearly and maintain a calm, controlled tone, regardless of the situation.
  • Rate of Speech: Speak at a prescribed, even rateâ€”not too fast and not too slow.
  • Microphone Positioning: Understand the correct distance and angle to hold the microphone to avoid distortion or faint transmissions.
  • Plain Language: Use clear phrases and pronunciations. Avoid slang and unnecessary jargon.

Radio Discipline: When NOT to Transmit

Maintaining clear airwaves is a critical component of radio discipline.

  • Wait for a Clear Channel: Do not transmit if a dispatcher or another unit asks you to stand by.
  • Avoid Long Messages: Keep transmissions concise. If a long message is unavoidable, let dispatch know first.
  • Respect Emergency Traffic: If a critical incident is unfolding elsewhere, do not transmit unless absolutely necessary. Your traffic can interfere with life-or-death communications.
  • Stay Professional: Do not use the radio for unnecessary traffic unrelated to official duties or unit availability.
