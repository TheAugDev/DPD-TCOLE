Family Violence & Related Offenses

Understanding Dynamics, Response, and Legal Protections

I. Dynamics of Family Violence

The Victim's Response

Victims of family violence often exhibit a complex set of behaviors due to trauma and stress. Officers should be aware of these common reactions:

  • Guilt and Self-Blame: Victims often feel responsible for the violence, especially if children witnessed it.
  • Mistrust: They may not trust anyone, including law enforcement, to provide help.
  • Denial: Some victims may deny the violence occurred or minimize its severity as a coping mechanism.
  • Erratic Behavior: Severe stress can cause victims to behave erratically or question their own sanity.

Profile of a Family Violence Offender

Offenders often share common characteristics that drive their behavior:

  • Low Self-Opinion: They often project their low self-esteem onto their victims.
  • Cycle of Violence: It's common for offenders to have grown up in violent homes themselves.
  • Public vs. Private Persona: Many offenders act charming in public but are violent at home.
  • Lack of Responsibility: They rarely take responsibility for their actions, often believing they are entitled to act as they do.
  • Other Behaviors: Substance abuse, aggressive sexual behavior, and extreme mood swings are common.

Types of Abuse

Physical Abuse

Any act causing physical pain.

Emotional Abuse

Includes ignoring, withholding affection, humiliation, isolation, and verbal insults.

Sexual Abuse

Includes rape, forced sexual acts, and refusal to use birth control.

II. Law Enforcement Response & Procedures

On-Scene Investigation

Family violence calls are volatile and dangerous. A proper response is crucial for safety and effective investigation.

  • Officer Response: At least two officers should respond. Approach should be quiet (no lights/sirens) and careful.
  • Entry: Officers may enter with consent from a resident or without consent if exigent circumstances (e.g., sounds of violence) exist.
  • Priorities: The immediate priority is to stop any ongoing violence and secure the scene.
  • Investigation: Interview parties separately, secure any weapons, and document the scene thoroughly with photos and notes.
  • Identifying the Predominant Aggressor: This is a key task. Officers should look at witness statements, relative size of the parties, severity of injuries, and any history of violence. Defensive injuries (like scratches on forearms) are an important clue.

Protective Orders (Texas Family Code)

A protective order is a civil court order designed to stop violence and prevent future harm.

  • Who Can Apply (FC 82.002): A family member, a member of a dating relationship, or a prosecuting attorney on behalf of a victim.
  • Court's Decision (FC 85.001): A court must issue a protective order if it finds that family violence has occurred AND is likely to occur in the future.
  • Prohibited Actions (FC 85.021, 85.022): The order can prohibit the offender from removing a child, disposing of property, coming near the victim's home/work/school, and possessing a firearm. It can also require counseling or a battering intervention program.
  • Duration (FC 85.025): A protective order is effective for two years unless the court specifies otherwise.
  • Officer Access (FC 86.001): Law enforcement agencies MUST establish procedures to ensure officers have access to information about protective orders during their duties.

Key Term Flashcards

Family Violence

The legal term in Texas for violence that occurs in the home or between family/household members. "Domestic violence" is not used in Texas law.

Predominant Aggressor

(Click to Flip)

The individual who initiates a violent episode. Identifying this person is crucial to avoid mistakenly arresting a victim who was acting in self-defense.

A civil court order that prohibits an offender from committing certain acts, such as contacting the victim or possessing a firearm, to prevent future family violence.
