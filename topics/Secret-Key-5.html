Secret Key #5: Have a Plan for Guessing

Maximizing Your Score When You're Unsure

I. The Three-Step Guessing Plan

1. When to Start Guessing

Knowing when to stop trying to solve a question and start guessing is a critical time-management skill.

  • Give it a Shot: First, genuinely try to answer the question from your knowledge. Focus on keywords and try to jog your memory.
  • Decide Quickly: Once you realize you don't know the answer, commit to guessing immediately. It's better to start the guessing process too early than too late.
  • Why it Works: It only takes a few seconds to recall something you know. The process of carefully eliminating wrong answers takes longer. Starting this process can even help jog your memory.

2. How to Narrow Down Choices

Your odds of guessing correctly increase dramatically with every answer choice you can eliminate.

The $5 Challenge

To avoid accidentally eliminating the correct answer, use this thought exercise: Only eliminate an answer choice if you are willing to bet $5 that it is wrong. This holds your decision to a higher standard and reduces careless eliminations.

Conversely, if you are willing to bet $5 that an answer is correct, select it and move on.

3. Which Answer to Choose

Test writers intentionally create plausible but incorrect answer choices to trap guessers. To avoid this trap, you need a predetermined plan.

It separates your knowledge (what you eliminated) from your impression (what "looks" right).

II. Bonus: Test-Taking Strategies

Question Strategies

  • Read Carefully: Read the entire question and all answer choices before selecting. Don't miss key words.
  • Use Contextual Clues: If you don't know a word, use the surrounding text to infer its meaning.
  • Watch for Hedge & Switchback Words: Be alert for words like "may," "often," and "sometimes" (hedges) or "but," "however," and "although" (switchbacks), as they change the meaning of a sentence.
  • Accept Face Value: Don't read too much into a question or make wild assumptions. The answer should be based on logic and the information given.

Answer Choice Strategies

  • Answer Choice Families: If two answers are direct opposites, one of them is often correct.
  • Avoid Fact Traps: An answer choice can be a true statement but not actually answer the question being asked. Stay focused.
  • Beware of Extreme Statements: Choices that use definitive words like "always" or "never" are often incorrect.
  • Use a Benchmark: When you find a good answer, use it as a standard to compare against the others. If a better one comes along, it becomes the new benchmark.
  • Predict the Answer: Try to formulate the answer in your head before looking at the choices. This helps you avoid being tricked by distractors.
