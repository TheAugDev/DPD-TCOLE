Victims of Crime

Understanding Trauma, Recovery, and Notification

I. The Victim's Experience

The Crisis Reaction

The reaction to a traumatic event is remarkably similar across all people and involves both physical and mental components.

Physical Response (Fight-or-Flight)

  • Adrenaline saturates the bloodstream.
  • Increased heart and breathing rates.
  • Sharpened senses.
  • Can lead to vomiting or defecation.
  • Followed by debilitating effects from adrenaline withdrawal.

Mental Response (Three Stages)

  1. Shock & Disbelief: Inability to organize thoughts; denial of the event's severity.
  2. Emotional Cataclysm: A whirlwind of intense emotions like terror, sorrow, and anger.
  3. Return to Equilibrium: The brain begins to integrate the experience, restoring emotional balance.

Recovery From Trauma

The ability to recover depends on internal and external factors. The reaction to crime can be broken down into three phases:

  1. Impact: The crime itself occurs.
  2. Recoil: The victim's emotional trajectory changes as a result of the crime.
  3. Reorganization: The victim's efforts to restore balance and order to their life.

Recovery is also influenced by the severity of the event and the ability to comprehend and articulate what happened. Helping a victim make sense of the event is a key part of healing.

Secondary Victimization

This occurs when the victim is mistreated or neglected after the crime, aggravating the original trauma. It can be unintentional but is devastating.

  • Sources: Can come from the criminal justice system, media, or even the medical community.
  • Officer's Role: Officers must avoid any behavior or comments that imply guilt on the part of the victim (e.g., "You shouldnâ€™t have been walking alone.").
  • Impact: It discourages victims from seeking further help and can cause immense psychological damage.
  • Secondary Victims: Family and friends of the immediate victim are also affected and need support.

II. Death Notification Protocol

Guidelines for Making a Notification

Notifying next of kin about a death is one of the most difficult duties an officer performs. It must be done with sensitivity and professionalism.

  1. Be Timely: Notify as quickly as possible after the body has been identified.
  2. Go in Person: Never make a death notification over the phone.
  3. Be in Uniform: This conveys respect and the official nature of the visit.
  4. Notify an Adult: Always deliver the news to an adult.
  5. Be Direct and Simple: Use clear, thoughtful language.
  6. Provide Information: Answer questions as long as it doesn't compromise an ongoing investigation.
  7. Show Empathy: Convey genuine sympathy and remain with the family until you have permission to leave.
  8. Never Leave Grieving People Alone: Offer to help make immediate arrangements (e.g., call other family, clergy).
  9. Respect Privacy: Never speak to the media without explicit permission from the family.
