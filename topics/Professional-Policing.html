Professional Policing

Evolution, Models, and the Texas Context

I. The Evolution of Policing

Historical Development of Police Service

The role and methodology of policing have changed significantly over the last century, largely driven by technology and shifting philosophies.

  • Early 20th Century: Characterized by strong social connections between officers and the communities they served.
  • Impact of Technology: The introduction of radio and automobiles allowed for faster response times but also created distance between officers and the public as foot patrols became less common.
  • Mid-Century Issues: For a long period, law enforcement lacked self-critique and evaluation. The increased use of computers and officer rotations further severed community connections.
  • Recent Trends: To combat this disconnection, modern policing has seen an increase in evaluation research and a renewed focus on community outreach and engagement.

The History of Policing in Texas

Texas has a unique law enforcement history shaped by its vast territory and frontier past.

  • Pre-Republic: As a province of Mexico, the land was policed by the Spanish military.
  • Texas Republic: After 1836, policing was handled by a militia organized by Stephen F. Austin, which eventually evolved into the iconic Texas Rangers.
  • Elected Officials: A key feature of Texas policing is that Constables and Sheriffs are elected officials.
  • State Standards: All licensed Texas law enforcement officers must meet specific requirements, including firearms proficiency, as outlined in the Commission Rules.

II. Models of Policing

The Traditional Police Service Model

This model is primarily reactive and incident-driven.

  • Focus: Handling individual situations and events after they occur.
  • Performance Metrics: Success is measured by statistics like crime rates and arrest numbers.
  • Decision Making: Top-down approach, with decisions made by middle and upper management.
  • Structure: Often subdivided into specialized units (e.g., traffic, homicide) that are good at their specific tasks.

The Community Policing Model

This model represents a philosophical shift towards proactive and collaborative policing.

  • Focus: Proactive communication and partnership with citizens and local leaders.
  • Performance Metrics: Success is measured by factors like community morale, cleanliness, and reduced fear of crime.
  • Decision Making: Collaborative approach, soliciting input from the community.
  • Officer's Role: Officers are role models and problem-solvers who build long-term relationships by being assigned to the same district over time.
