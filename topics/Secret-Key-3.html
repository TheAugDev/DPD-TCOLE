Secret Key #3: Practice the Right Way

Turning Practice into Performance

I. The Purpose of Practice Tests

Diagnosing Your Weaknesses

Practice tests are one of the most effective ways to evaluate your progress because they show you exactly where you need to improve. After every practice test, you must analyze your performance by focusing on three groups of questions:

Questions You Got Wrong

Questions You Guessed On

Questions You Found Difficult

This analysis reveals your weak areas. Ask yourself *why* each question was a problem: Was it a lack of understanding, a forgotten vocabulary term, or a need for more practice to build speed? Use this insight to direct your review.

The "Study the Concept, Not the Question" Rule

Important Warning

Many practice tests have answer explanations. It's tempting to read the explanation and believe you understand the concept. However, an explanation only covers a small part of the broader topic. Even if it makes perfect sense, you must go back and investigate the entire related concept until you have a thorough understanding.

Memorizing practice questions is not helpful, as the actual test will have different questions. If you only know the right answers to sample questions, you won't be prepared. Study the concepts until you understand them fully, and you'll be able to answer any question that appears on the test.

II. The Practice Test Strategy

Training for the Mental Marathon

Test day is a mental marathon. You need to be prepared for answering questions, managing your time, and using test-taking strategies simultaneously. Like training for a real marathon, you should start slowly and work your way up to full intensity.

First, find a good source for practice tests. The official test website often provides sample tests or recommends study resources. If you have time, using multiple resources can be helpful to see concepts explained from different angles.

A Three-Step Practice Method

If you can find at least three practice tests, this progressive strategy is highly effective:

1

Untimed and Open-Book Practice

Take the first test with no time limit and all your notes available. Focus on applying the strategies you've learned and understanding the "why" behind each answer.

2

Timed and Open-Book Practice

Take the second test with your notes, but set a timer. This helps you practice pacing and retrieving information under pressure.

3

Timed and Closed-Book Practice

Take all remaining tests as if it were the real thing. Sit in a quiet room, put away all materials, and work as quickly and accurately as possible to simulate the actual test environment.
