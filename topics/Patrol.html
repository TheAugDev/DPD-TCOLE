Patrol Procedures & Consular Notification

Tactics, Safety, and Diplomatic Protocols

I. Patrol Fundamentals

Preparation and Knowledge

Effective patrol begins before the shift starts. An officer's preparedness is key to their effectiveness and safety.

  • Area Familiarity: Know the geography, socioeconomic characteristics, traffic patterns, and common crime types in your patrol area.
  • Pre-Shift Briefing: Be aware of special circumstances like stolen property, outstanding warrants, or planned events.
  • Personal and Equipment Readiness: Ensure your uniform is clean and your equipment is prepared and functional before every shift.

Patrol Patterns and Strategies

Varying patrol patterns prevents criminals from predicting officer movements.

Circular Pattern: Moving from the center of the beat outward, or from the perimeter inward.
Double-Back Pattern: Consistently returning to re-check areas, useful for learning a beat or focusing on problem spots.
Random Pattern: An unpredictable route that prevents criminals from anticipating police presence.

Patrol Types

  • Preventive Patrol: High visibility to deter crime.
  • Apprehension Patrol: Low visibility (concealed) for surveillance and catching criminals in the act.

Modes of Patrol

Each patrol mode has distinct advantages:

  • Automobile: Offers speed, visibility, and the ability to carry equipment. The most common mode.
  • Motorcycle: Allows for quick response but cannot transport suspects. Often used for traffic enforcement and escorts.
  • Foot/Bicycle: Enables personal contact with the community, building trust and gathering intelligence. Bicycles can access areas cars cannot.
  • Horseback: Provides excellent visibility and improves community relations, as citizens are often less hostile.
  • Helicopter: Covers a large area quickly for surveillance or searches.

One-Officer vs. Two-Officer Patrols

One-Officer Patrols:

  • Develops more competent and self-reliant officers.
  • Reduces personality conflicts.
  • Allows for greater patrol coverage with the same number of vehicles.

Two-Officer Patrols:

  • Considered safer for officers.
  • Better observation capabilities.
  • Easier to manage radio traffic while driving.
  • Provides a good training environment for newer officers.

II. Tactical Considerations and Officer Safety

Common Patrol Hazards

Officer safety depends on avoiding predictable and vulnerable situations.

  • Avoid Drawing Attention: Don't rev engines or slam doors when approaching a scene quietly.
  • Avoid Silhouetting: Do not stand in doorways or in front of open windows where you become an easy target against a light background.
  • Control Hands: Do not allow a suspect to put their hands in their pockets or remove them without your instruction. Palms-up is the safest position.
  • Light Discipline: Never point flashlights at fellow officers at night, as it gives away positions.

High-Risk Situations

High-Risk Vehicle Stops: Alert dispatch with location and description, request backup, use lights for illumination, park to the left, and use clear commands to have occupants exit one by one.

Safe Building Searches: Always have at least two officers enter, secure the perimeter, and use a systematic method like "leap-frogging" to clear rooms.

Bomb Threats: Evacuate if necessary, secure the area, request specialized assistance, and do NOT touch any suspicious objects. Turn off all radios within 500 feet.

Active Shooter / Homicide in Progress Response

The immediate objective is to save as many lives as possible by isolating, disabling, or killing the shooter. This requires immediate action, often with limited information and equipment.

Contact Team

Focuses on locating and neutralizing the suspect. Prioritizes stopping the threat over immediate victim aid.

Rescue Team

Focuses on locating and extracting the wounded and stranded to a safe location.

The Diamond Formation is a common four-officer tactical movement with a point man, left flank, right flank, and rear guard.

Crowd Management

The primary objectives are to arrest offenders and disperse the assembly. It is crucial to understand the type of crowd you are facing:

  • Casual Crowd: No common purpose or leadership.
  • Cohesive Crowd: Common purpose but no leadership.
  • Expressive Crowd: Has a purpose and leadership (e.g., protest); may be emotional but not necessarily hostile.
  • Aggressive Crowd: Has a purpose and leadership; likely to be violent and belligerent towards police.
  • Mob: A crowd with a single, often destructive, purpose (aggression, looting, escape).

III. Consular Notification

Arrest of a Foreign National

When a foreign national is arrested, officers have specific obligations under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations.

  1. Determine the arrestee's country of citizenship.
  2. Check if that country is on the "mandatory notification" list.
  3. If Mandatory: You MUST notify that country's consulate of the arrest without delay.
  4. If Not Mandatory: You MUST ask the arrestee, "Do you want me to notify your country's consular officials?" Record their answer.
  5. This rule applies to all foreign nationals, regardless of their immigration status.

Consular officials have the right to visit, call, or write to the detainee and can assist with legal representation.
