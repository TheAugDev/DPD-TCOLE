Family Code & Juvenile Issues

Understanding and Protecting Vulnerable Populations

I. Understanding Juvenile Behavior

Adolescent Encounters with Police

Understanding the psychological drivers behind adolescent behavior can help officers de-escalate encounters. Common factors include:

  • Need for Independence: Criminal activity can be an expression of rebellion against parental and societal restrictions.
  • Identity Expression: Defying authority can be a way for teens to define themselves.
  • Exploration: Breaking rules is sometimes a method of learning and testing boundaries.
  • Peer Pressure: Young people are highly susceptible to the influence of their peers, which can lead to group criminal activity.

Juvenile Gangs: Context and Motivation

Reasons for Joining:

Gangs often provide a sense of belonging, identity, economic opportunity, and excitement that may be missing from an adolescent's life, especially in communities with high unemployment and few positive institutions.

Stages of Gang Membership:

  1. Minor Acts: Small acts of violence or property destruction to prove sincerity.
  2. Image Makeover: Adopting gang colors, tattoos, and clothing.
  3. Serious Offense: Committing a serious crime to solidify membership and bond with the group.

II. Legal Procedures for Juveniles

Juvenile Custody & Detention

  • Taking into Custody (FC 52.01): An officer can take a child into custody with a court order or with probable cause that the child has violated a law or condition of probation.
  • Directive to Apprehend (FC 52.015): Law enforcement can request a juvenile court to issue this directive based on probable cause.
  • Non-Secure Custody (CCP 45.058): A child taken into custody must be held in an unlocked area (like a lobby or office), cannot be handcuffed to a stationary object, must be under constant visual supervision, and cannot be held for more than six hours.
  • Parent Notification (FC 52.04): When a child is referred to juvenile court, the law enforcement agency must notify the parents as soon as possible with a full statement of the alleged conduct.

Juvenile Rights & Statements

  • Delinquent Conduct (FC 51.03): Defined as a violation of a penal law, court order, or the Alcoholic Beverage Code. Also includes running away from home or excessive school absences.
  • Waiver of Rights (FC 51.09): A child and their attorney can waive rights, but it must be voluntary, in writing, and with full understanding of the consequences.
  • Admissibility of Statements (FC 51.095): A child's statement is admissible only if they have been advised of their rights, had an opportunity for counsel, and made the statement in the presence of a magistrate who confirms their understanding.
  • Fingerprinting/Photographing (FC 58.002): Cannot be done without juvenile court consent, unless the child is in custody for a felony or jailable misdemeanor. Consent from a parent is required for children not in custody.

III. Child Protection & Missing Persons

Child Abuse & Neglect Reporting

  • Mandatory Reporting (FC 261.101): ANYONE who has reason to believe a child is being abused or neglected MUST make a report. Professionals have 48 hours to report.
  • Failure to Report (FC 261.109): This is a Class A Misdemeanor.
  • False Report (FC 261.107): Knowingly making a false report is a State Jail Felony.
  • Immunity (FC 261.106): A person who reports in good faith is immune from civil or criminal liability.
  • Privileged Communication (FC 261.202): Privilege (e.g., doctor-patient) does not apply in child abuse proceedings, except for attorney-client privilege.

Missing Children & AMBER Alert

Immediate Investigation (CCP 63.009): Upon receiving a report of a missing child, a law enforcement agency MUST immediately start an investigation and enter the child's name into the state clearinghouse and NCIC.

AMBER Alert System (Gov. Code 411.355): The Department of Public Safety activates the system at the request of local law enforcement if:

  • The abducted child is 13 years of age or younger.
  • The agency believes the child is in immediate danger of serious bodily injury or death.
  • There is sufficient information available to disseminate to the public to assist in the recovery.
