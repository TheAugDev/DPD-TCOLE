Problem Solving & Critical Thinking

Essential Skills for Modern Policing

I. Effective Communication Strategies

Creating a Climate for Communication

Effective communication goes beyond words. An officer's physical presence and awareness of the environment are crucial.

  • Setting: Make the environment as comfortable and peaceful as possible.
  • Personal Space (Proxemics): Be mindful of distance. Stand close enough to hear easily but far enough to respect personal boundaries and ensure safety.
  • Eye Contact: Maintain consistent, but not continuous, eye contact to show sincere interest without being intimidating.
  • Posture: Good posture conveys confidence and authority. Leaning forward slightly can signify particular interest.

Reading Nonverbal Cues (Kinesics)

Facial expressions and body language provide a wealth of information, often revealing more than words.

Facial Expressions:

  • Flared Nostrils: Arousal (emotional or sexual).
  • Pursed Lips: Disagreement or frustration.
  • Raised Upper Lip (Snarl): Contempt or repulsion.
  • Dilated Pupils: High emotion or perception of a threat.
  • Looking Down: Shame or sheepishness.

Body Language:

  • Pointing: Often perceived as a threat or challenge.
  • Palms Up Gestures: Appears friendly and welcoming.
  • Crossed Arms: Can indicate defensiveness (tightly held) or relaxation (loosely held).
  • Feet Pointing Out: Suggests dominance.
  • Feet Pointing In: Suggests submission.

Listening Skills & Vocal Cues

Active Listening: This is a crucial skill. It involves not just hearing, but confirming understanding by paraphrasing, asking clarifying questions, and paying attention to the speaker's emotional state. Repeating what the speaker says can be a simple but powerful tool.

Paralinguistics: These are aspects of vocal production other than words, such as volume, tone, and rhythm. A person's tone of voice is a strong indicator of their emotional state.

Haptics: This refers to communication through touch. A light touch on an extremity can convey affection, while a forceful touch may indicate aggression or dominance. Officers must be aware of when physical contact is and is not appropriate.

II. Critical Thinking & Problem Solving Frameworks

The Critical Thinking Process

Critical thinking is the analysis, evaluation, and interpretation of information. It requires an officer to:

  • Maintain Skepticism: Do not accept claims without supporting evidence.
  • Evaluate Arguments: Look for holes in logic, even in arguments you agree with.
  • Recognize Bias: Evaluate your own prejudices and the assumptions of others.
  • Empathize: Try to see the world from other perspectives to fully understand the situation.

Problem-Solving Models

Structured models help officers move from identifying a problem to implementing an effective solution.

The SARA Model

  1. Scanning: Identify and prioritize recurring problems.
  2. Analysis: Gather information from multiple sources to understand the underlying causes of the problem.
  3. Response: Develop and implement a plan of action designed to solve the problem.
  4. Assessment: Evaluate the impact of the response to determine its effectiveness and make adjustments.

The Crime Triangle

This model helps analyze problems by focusing on the three elements required for a crime to occur: an Offender, a Victim, and a Location. Removing any one of these elements can prevent the crime.
