Crisis Intervention & Mental Health Code

Legal and Tactical Considerations

I. CIT Principles & Legal Framework

Origins and Intentions of CIT

Crisis Intervention Training (CIT) was developed in Memphis, TN, after a tragic incident involving the death of a mentally disturbed man. The primary goal of CIT is to provide officers with effective strategies to de-escalate situations involving individuals in crisis, separating mental illness from the criminal justice system whenever possible.

The Control Paradox

A core CIT concept: officers often gain more control over a situation by using less force. De-escalation and communication are key to resolving conflicts with disturbed individuals peacefully and safely.

Texas Education Code

Sec. 37.107 - Trespass on School Grounds: A Class C misdemeanor.

Sec. 37.123 - Intentional Disruptive Behavior: Illegally seizing control of a school building, preventing a lawful assembly, or obstructing a hallway. A Class B misdemeanor.

Sec. 37.124 - Disrupting Classes: Intentionally disrupting class or other school activities. A Class C misdemeanor.

Sec. 37.125 - Firearm on School Property: Exhibiting, using, or threatening to use a firearm on school property or a school bus. A third-degree felony.

Sec. 37.126 - Disrupting Transportation: Intentionally disrupting the lawful transportation of children on a school bus. A Class C misdemeanor.

Code of Criminal Procedure - Victim's Rights

Art. 56.02 (Rights of Crime Victims): Victims have the right to protection, to have their safety considered in bail settings, to be informed of proceedings, and to provide testimony at sentencing.

Art. 56.04 (Crime Victim Liaison): Law enforcement agencies must appoint a liaison to ensure victims' rights are upheld.

Art. 56.045 (Sexual Assault Victims): Victims have the right to have a special advocate present during forensic medical exams.

Art. 56.07 (Victim Notification): Law enforcement must provide victims with written notice about emergency services and their rights.

Art. 42.21 (Family Violence Notification): A reasonable attempt must be made to notify the victim before a person convicted of a family violence offense is released.

II. Major Court Decisions on Use of Force

Graham v. Connor (1989)

This is the landmark case establishing the "Objective Reasonableness" standard. An officer's use of force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, based on the facts known at that moment, without regard to 20/20 hindsight. The officer's underlying intent or motivation is not the determining factor.

Young v. City of Killeen

The court found an officer acted negligently by creating a situation that led to the use of deadly force. The officer ordered a driver out of the car, and when the driver reached down (potentially for a weapon), the officer shot him. The court ruled the officer should have used other tactics first.

Saucier v. Katz (2001) & Milstead v. Kibler (2001)

These cases helped define "Qualified Immunity." An officer is protected from liability unless their conduct violates a clearly established constitutional or statutory right that a reasonable person would have known about.

Okonkwo v. Fernandez (2003)

Reinforced the concept of qualified immunity in Texas, stating that an officer must explicitly plead for it in court to be protected from suit and liability for actions a reasonable person would not have known were illegal.
