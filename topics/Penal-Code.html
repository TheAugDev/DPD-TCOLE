The Texas Penal Code

A Comprehensive Review of Offenses and Punishments

I. General Principles & Culpability

General Provisions (Ch. 1-3)

  • Sec. 1.02 (Objectives): To establish penalties, ensure public safety, define offenses, and protect non-criminal behavior.
  • Sec. 1.04 (Territorial Jurisdiction): Texas has jurisdiction over offenses committed within its borders or attempts to commit offenses within the state.
  • Sec. 2.01 (Burden of Proof): All persons are presumed innocent until guilt is proven beyond a reasonable doubt.
  • Sec. 3.01 (Criminal Episode): The commission of two or more offenses in the same transaction or as part of a common scheme.

Culpability & Responsibility (Ch. 6-7)

An offense requires a voluntary act and a culpable mental state. The four mental states, from highest to lowest, are:

  1. Intentionally: Conscious objective to engage in the conduct or cause the result.
  2. Knowingly: Aware that conduct is reasonably certain to cause the result.
  3. Recklessly: Aware of but consciously disregards a substantial and unjustifiable risk.
  4. Criminal Negligence: Ought to be aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk.

A person can also be held criminally responsible for the conduct of another if they act as a party to the offense (Sec 7.02).

Defenses to Culpability (Ch. 8)

  • Sec. 8.01 (Insanity): Affirmative defense if a severe mental defect prevented knowing the conduct was wrong.
  • Sec. 8.04 (Voluntary Intoxication): Not a defense to guilt, but can be used to mitigate punishment.
  • Sec. 8.05 (Duress): Affirmative defense if compelled by a threat of imminent death or serious bodily injury.
  • Sec. 8.06 (Entrapment): Defense if induced by law enforcement using persuasion likely to cause the offense.
  • Sec. 8.07 (Age): A person generally cannot be prosecuted for an offense committed when younger than 15.

II. Offenses & Punishments

Punishments (Ch. 12)

Misdemeanors:

  • Class A: Up to 1 year in jail, $4,000 fine.
  • Class B: Up to 180 days in jail, $2,000 fine.
  • Class C: Up to $500 fine.

Felonies:

  • Capital: Life without parole or death.
  • 1st Degree: 5-99 years or life, $10,000 fine.
  • 2nd Degree: 2-20 years, $10,000 fine.
  • 3rd Degree: 2-10 years, $10,000 fine.
  • State Jail: 180 days - 2 years, $10,000 fine.

Preparatory (Inchoate) Offenses (Ch. 15)

  • Criminal Attempt (Sec. 15.01): Taking a substantial step toward an offense. Generally punished one category lower than the target offense.
  • Criminal Conspiracy (Sec. 15.02): Agreeing with others to commit a felony and one person performs an overt act. Punished one category lower than the target offense.
  • Criminal Solicitation (Sec. 15.03): Requesting or commanding another to commit a capital or first-degree felony.

Offenses Against Persons (Ch. 19-25)

Includes Criminal Homicide (Ch. 19), Kidnapping and Unlawful Restraint (Ch. 20), Sexual Offenses (Ch. 21), Assaultive Offenses (Ch. 22), and Offenses Against the Family (Ch. 25).

Offenses Against Property (Ch. 28-35)

Includes Arson and Criminal Mischief (Ch. 28), Robbery (Ch. 29), Burglary and Criminal Trespass (Ch. 30), Theft (Ch. 31), and Fraud (Ch. 32-35).

Offenses Against Public Order & Administration (Ch. 36-49, 71)

A broad category including Bribery (Ch. 36), Perjury (Ch. 37), Obstruction of Governmental Operation (Ch. 38), Disorderly Conduct (Ch. 42), Prostitution (Ch. 43), Weapons Offenses (Ch. 46), Gambling (Ch. 47), and Intoxication Offenses (Ch. 49). Engaging in Organized Criminal Activity (Ch. 71) enhances the punishment for underlying crimes.

Awareness that one's conduct is of a certain nature or that a specific result is reasonably certain to occur.

A person ought to be aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk, but fails to perceive it.

An act that is more than mere preparation, done with the specific intent to commit an offense, but failing to complete it.

Robbery

(Click to Flip)

Causing or threatening bodily injury to another person during the course of committing theft.

Burglary

(Click to Flip)

Unlawfully entering a building or habitation with the intent to commit a felony, theft, or assault.

Capital Murder

(Click to Flip)

Murder plus specific aggravating circumstances, such as killing a police officer, killing during another felony, or killing a child under 6.
