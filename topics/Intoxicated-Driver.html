Intoxicated Driver & SFST

Legal Standards and Field Procedures

I. Legal Framework: Implied Consent & Specimen Collection

Implied Consent (Transportation Code Ch. 724)

Texas operates under an "implied consent" law, meaning that by operating a motor vehicle in a public place, a person is considered to have consented to provide a breath or blood specimen if arrested for a DWI-related offense.

  • Sec. 724.011: Establishes the principle of implied consent upon arrest for operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated.
  • Sec. 724.012: An officer may request a specimen if a person is arrested for an offense under Penal Code Chapter 49. The officer selects the type of test.
  • Sec. 724.013: A person may refuse to provide a specimen if they have not been arrested.
  • Sec. 724.017: Blood specimens must be taken under sanitary conditions by qualified professionals (physicians, nurses, chemists, etc.).

Statutory Warnings & Consequences (Sec. 724.015)

Before requesting a specimen, an officer MUST inform the suspect of the consequences of refusing or failing the test.

Required Warnings (DIC-24):

  • Refusal can be used against them in court.
  • Refusal will result in an automatic driver's license suspension of at least 180 days.
  • If they provide a specimen and fail (0.08+ BAC for adults), their license will be suspended for at least 90 days.
  • For individuals under 21, any detectable amount of alcohol results in a license suspension of at least 60 days.

Procedure for Refusal (Sec. 724.032)

If a suspect refuses to provide a specimen after being given the statutory warnings, the officer must:

  1. Have the person sign a refusal statement (if possible).
  2. Immediately serve a notice of license suspension.
  3. Take possession of the person's current driver's license.
  4. Submit a written report of the refusal to the director of DPS.

II. Field Investigation & Sobriety Testing

DWI Detection: Clues and Observation

Detecting an intoxicated driver involves observing cues during three phases: vehicle in motion, personal contact, and pre-arrest screening.

  • Vehicle in Motion: Weaving, drifting, erratic speed changes, driving on the wrong side of the road, stopping improperly at signals.
  • Personal Contact: Bloodshot eyes, disheveled clothing, slurred speech, odor of alcohol, fumbling with wallet/license.
  • Probable Cause: Reasonable suspicion is needed for the initial stop, but probable cause is required for an arrest. The presence of open containers or a driver's inability to walk in a straight line can contribute to probable cause.

Standardized Field Sobriety Tests (SFSTs)

SFSTs are a battery of standardized tests used to assess a driver's impairment. Officers must be familiar with the three validated tests, including their procedures and how to interpret the results (clues) to assess nystagmus and divided attention skills.

Officer Considerations:

Officers must exercise caution and not endanger a highly intoxicated person with physical tests. They must also be aware that conditions like diabetic shock or epilepsy can mimic signs of intoxication.

Key Term Flashcards

Implied Consent

A legal principle (TC 724.011) that states by driving on a public road, a person has agreed to submit to a chemical test if arrested for a DWI offense.

SFST

(Click to Flip)

Standardized Field Sobriety Tests. A battery of three validated tests used to determine impairment: Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus, Walk-and-Turn, and One-Leg Stand.

Nystagmus

(Click to Flip)

An involuntary jerking of the eyes. Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus (HGN) is exaggerated by alcohol consumption and is a key indicator in SFSTs.
