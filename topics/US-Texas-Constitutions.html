U.S. & Texas Constitutions, Bill of Rights, and CJS

Foundations of American and Texas Law

I. The U.S. Constitution & Bill of Rights

Structure and Key Principles

  • Writ of Habeas Corpus: A foundational right requiring a person under arrest to be brought before a judge or into court, to prevent unlawful detention.
  • Article 2: Outlines the procedure for electing the President and Vice President, including eligibility and the Electoral College.
  • Article 3: Establishes the Supreme Court and defines its original and appellate jurisdiction. It also defines treason, the only crime listed in the Constitution.

Key Constitutional Amendments

The Bill of Rights and other key amendments guarantee fundamental freedoms and legal protections:

  • 1st Amendment: Freedom of speech, religion, press, assembly, and petition.
  • 4th Amendment: Protection against unreasonable searches and seizures; requires probable cause for warrants.
  • 5th Amendment: Protection against double jeopardy and self-incrimination; guarantees due process.
  • 6th Amendment: Right to a speedy trial, legal counsel, and to confront one's accusers.
  • 8th Amendment: Prohibits excessive bail, excessive fines, and cruel and unusual punishment.
  • 9th Amendment: Establishes that individuals have rights beyond those explicitly listed in the Constitution.
  • 10th Amendment: Establishes the rights of states.
  • 14th Amendment: Contains the Supremacy Clause, making federal law supreme over state law, and guarantees equal protection.

The Constitutional Role of Police

The Constitution and Bill of Rights envision peace officers not as tools to take away rights, but as guardians who ensure fairness and protect the inalienable rights of all citizens. This requires officers to:

  • Create conditions of fairness and equality.
  • Recognize and work to eliminate prejudice.
  • Learn and actively protect the rights of citizens.
  • Stay informed about new legislation and evolving legal interpretations.

II. The Texas Constitution

Article 1: The Texas Bill of Rights

Similar to the U.S. Bill of Rights, Article 1 of the Texas Constitution outlines fundamental individual rights:

  • Sec. 2: All political power resides in the people.
  • Sec. 3 & 4: All citizens have equal rights and are entitled to equal treatment under the law.
  • Sec. 6 & 8: Guarantees freedom of worship, speech, and the press.
  • Sec. 9 & 10: Protects against unlawful searches and seizures and enumerates the rights of the accused (right to counsel, habeas corpus, etc.).
  • Sec. 23: The right to keep and bear arms.
  • Sec. 30: Outlines the rights of crime victims.

Article 17: Amending the Constitution

The process for amending the Texas Constitution is distinct from the federal process:

  1. An amendment is proposed by the legislature, requiring a two-thirds majority vote.
  2. The proposed amendment is published in newspapers across the state.
  3. It is then voted on by the public in the next general election.
  4. The amendment passes with a simple majority vote from the electorate.
  5. The governor ratifies the approved amendment.

III. The Criminal Justice System & Texas Courts

The Three Components of the CJS

The criminal justice system is made up of three interrelated parts. Actions in one component directly impact the others.

  1. Police: City, county, state, and federal law enforcement agencies.
  2. Courts: Municipal, county, state, and federal court systems.
  3. Corrections: Jails, prisons, probation, parole, and fines.

Texas Court Jurisdictions

  • Municipal Courts: Exclusive jurisdiction over violations of city ordinances; concurrent jurisdiction with Justice Courts over cases punishable by fine only.
  • Justice Courts: Original jurisdiction in criminal cases punishable by fine only.
  • County Courts: Original jurisdiction for misdemeanors where the fine exceeds $500.
  • District Courts: Original jurisdiction for all felonies and misdemeanors involving official misconduct.
  • Courts of Appeals: Appellate jurisdiction in their districts for all cases except those involving the death penalty.
  • Court of Criminal Appeals: The highest criminal court in Texas, with final appellate jurisdiction in all criminal cases.
